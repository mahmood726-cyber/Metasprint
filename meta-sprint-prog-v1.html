Module 1: Prognostic Factor Fundamentals (10 min)

Prognostic vs Predictive Factors

A prognostic factor is associated with outcome regardless of treatment (e.g., tumour stage predicts survival). A predictive factor modifies treatment effect (e.g., EGFR mutation predicts response to a specific drug). The distinction matters for clinical use and study design.

Common Study Designs:

  • Cohort study: Follow-up from exposure/diagnosis to outcome — gold standard for prognosis
  • Case-control: Efficient for rare outcomes; recall and selection bias risks
  • Nested case-control: Cases and controls drawn from a defined cohort — reduces bias

Key Effect Measures:

  • HR (Hazard Ratio): For time-to-event outcomes (survival, recurrence) — most common
  • OR (Odds Ratio): For binary outcomes in case-control designs
  • RR (Risk Ratio): For binary outcomes in cohort designs with cumulative incidence
  • Adjusted vs unadjusted: Multivariate models (Cox regression, logistic) control for confounders — always prefer adjusted estimates when available
⚠️ PROGRESS Framework: The PROGRESS framework (PROGnosis RESearch Strategy) provides a taxonomy for prognostic research: (1) fundamental prognosis, (2) prognostic factor research, (3) prognostic model research, (4) predictive factor research. Clarify which domain your review addresses before starting.

Module 2: Prognostic Model Development & Validation (10 min)

Steps in Model Development

Prognostic models combine multiple factors to predict individual risk. Development involves: (1) specifying candidate predictors, (2) handling missing data, (3) fitting a regression model, (4) assessing performance, and (5) presenting usable output (score/nomogram).

Validation Strategies:

  • Internal validation: Bootstrap or cross-validation on the same dataset — required before any external use
  • External validation: Applying the model to a different (temporal, geographic, or institutional) dataset — essential for generalisability
Performance MetricWhat It MeasuresInterpretation
C-statistic / AUCDiscrimination — separating high vs low risk0.5 = no discrimination; 1.0 = perfect
Calibration plotAgreement between predicted and observed riskPoints on diagonal = well calibrated
Calibration slopeSystematic over/under-prediction1.0 = ideal; <1 = overfitting
Net ReclassificationImprovement in risk category assignmentPositive = better than comparator
🚫 Overfitting Risk: Models with many predictors relative to events are overfit — optimism-corrected estimates via bootstrap shrinkage (Harrell's heuristic: ≥10 events per variable). Apply shrinkage factors or ridge/LASSO penalisation. Report TRIPOD checklist items for transparent model reporting.

Module 3: Pooling Prognostic Evidence Across Studies (10 min)

Key Challenges in Prognostic Meta-Analysis

Prognostic evidence is notoriously heterogeneous: studies differ in adjustment sets, cutpoints for continuous factors, follow-up duration, outcome definitions, and study populations. These differences complicate direct pooling.

Unadjusted vs Adjusted Estimates: Unadjusted HRs overestimate the independent prognostic value of a factor. Pool adjusted estimates where reported; sensitivity-analyse unadjusted-only when adjusted are sparse.

ApproachWhen to UseKey Consideration
Random-effects MA (aggregate)Studies report summary HRs/ORsAccount for between-study heterogeneity in tau²
IPD meta-analysisIndividual patient data availableStandardise cutpoints; one-stage or two-stage
Converting HR/OR/RRMixed effect measures across studiesUse baseline risk to convert; log-scale pooling

💡 Cutpoint Heterogeneity:
1. Continuous factor dichotomised at different thresholds inflates heterogeneity
2. Where possible, obtain adjusted HR per unit or per SD increase
3. Consider meta-regression on cutpoint value if data allow
4. IPD allows re-analysis with a uniform cutpoint across all studies

Module 4: Risk of Bias in Prognostic Studies (5 min)

QUIPS Tool (Quality In Prognosis Studies)

QUIPS assesses six domains for prognostic factor studies. Each domain is rated Low / Moderate / High risk of bias based on signalling questions.

QUIPS DomainCore QuestionCommon Issue
Study participationIs the sample representative of the target population?Referral bias, exclusions not described
Study attritionIs loss to follow-up acceptably low and non-differential?>20% dropout with unknown reasons
Prognostic factor measurementIs the factor measured reliably and consistently?Self-reported data, no validation of assay
Outcome measurementIs the outcome measured reliably, blinded to factor status?Unblinded adjudication of soft endpoints
ConfoundingAre important confounders identified and adjusted?Key confounders omitted from model
Statistical analysisIs analysis appropriate and fully reported?Cutpoint optimisation without correction
⚠️ Reporting Adequacy: Many prognostic studies report only significant results (positive publication bias) or omit adjustment details. Rate "Statistical analysis" domain High if the adjustment set is not specified or if stepwise selection without correction was used.

Module 5: Interpreting & Reporting Results (5 min)

GRADE for Prognosis

Certainty of prognostic evidence starts at High (for prospective cohort studies) and is downgraded for: risk of bias, inconsistency, imprecision, indirectness, and publication bias. Unlike intervention reviews, dose-response is not an upgrading criterion.

Absolute vs Relative Prognostic Effects:

  • Relative (HR/RR): useful for comparing factor strength across populations
  • Absolute risk difference at a given time-point: needed for clinical decision-making
  • Always apply relative estimates to a clinically plausible baseline risk
Reporting GuidelineApplies ToKey Items
REMARKTumour marker / biomarker studiesCutpoint justification, full multivariate model
TRIPODPrognostic model development/validationSample size, missing data, calibration plot
PROGRESSAll prognostic factor researchPre-specification, adjusted analysis
🚫 Common Pitfalls:
• Immortal time bias: Time before exposure classification counted in follow-up — inflates HR artificially
• Competing risks: Death before the outcome of interest; standard Kaplan-Meier overestimates cumulative incidence
• Decision curve analysis: Assess net benefit across threshold probabilities before claiming clinical utility
• Cutpoint optimisation without correction: Inflates significance — always penalise or report optimism-corrected estimates

Story One
The Overfitting Illusion
"C-statistic 0.92! Our model is nearly perfect..." But will it work in new patients?
🌅 The Beginning

A prediction model for cardiac risk was developed using 15 predictors in 200 patients with 30 events. Development c-statistic: 0.92. The authors declared: "Excellent discrimination!"

Hospitals adopted it. Guidelines recommended it. Thousands of patients were risk-stratified using this "nearly perfect" model.

❓ The Question You Must Ask
With 30 events and 15 predictors, that's only 2 events per variable. Did they perform internal validation? External validation?
🔍 The Hidden Truth

A systematic review with external validation found:

  • Bootstrap validation: Optimism-corrected c-statistic = 0.71 (not 0.92!)
  • External validation #1: c-statistic = 0.63
  • External validation #2: c-statistic = 0.58 (nearly useless)
  • Calibration: Predicted 30% risk actually had 10% event rate

The "excellent" model was severely overfitted. Real-world performance was barely better than chance.

⚖️ Two Paths
❌ What They Did

Reported only apparent performance. No internal validation. No external validation. Too many predictors for events.

Result: False confidence. Patients misclassified. Wrong treatment decisions.

✅ What META-SPRINT Would Do

Check events per variable (need ≥10). Require bootstrap validation. Prioritize external validations. Pool validation c-statistics separately.

Result: Realistic performance. Proper model selection. Better patient care.

📜 The Lesson
Development c-statistics are almost always optimistic. External validation is the true test. Always check events per variable. Never trust a model without independent validation.
🤔 Reflect: Have you pooled development and validation c-statistics separately? Have you assessed overfitting risk?
Story Two
The Adjustment Paradox
"Unadjusted HR shows strong effect! But wait, the adjusted HR is null..." Which one is right?
🌅 The Beginning

A prognostic factor meta-analysis pooled 20 studies of tumor marker X and survival. Unadjusted HR: 2.8 (95% CI: 2.2-3.6). Highly significant! Marker X predicts death!

Laboratories added the test. Oncologists used it for treatment decisions. Patients were told "your marker is elevated."

❓ The Question You Must Ask
What happens after adjusting for tumor stage? Is marker X truly independent, or is it just a proxy for advanced disease?
🔍 The Hidden Truth

Stratified analysis by adjustment level revealed:

  • Unadjusted (12 studies): HR = 2.8 [2.2-3.6] — Strong effect!
  • Adjusted for stage (5 studies): HR = 1.4 [1.0-2.0] — Modest effect
  • Fully adjusted (3 studies): HR = 1.1 [0.8-1.5] — NULL EFFECT
  • The reason: Marker X simply correlates with tumor stage

The unadjusted HR was confounded. Marker X added nothing beyond what staging already told us.

⚖️ Two Paths
❌ What They Did

Pooled all studies together. Mixed unadjusted and adjusted. Concluded marker X is prognostic.

Result: Expensive testing. No clinical value. Patients misled about their prognosis.

✅ What META-SPRINT Would Do

Pool unadjusted and adjusted separately. Stratify by adjustment set. Report independent prognostic value. Use PROBAST.

Result: Clear answer: marker adds nothing. Saved costs. Honest patient communication.

📜 The Lesson
Unadjusted and adjusted HRs can tell opposite stories. Always pool them separately. A prognostic factor is only useful if it adds value beyond existing predictors.
🤔 Reflect: Have you stratified by adjustment level? Have you asked whether your factor adds incremental value?
Story Three
The Validation Gap
"The model works beautifully in European hospitals..." But what about Africa and Asia?
🌅 The Beginning

A sepsis prediction model was developed and validated in 15 European ICUs. Meta-analysis of validations showed: pooled c-statistic 0.82. Guidelines recommended global adoption.

WHO endorsed it for low-resource settings. Hospitals in Africa and Asia implemented it. Treatment decisions followed its predictions.

❓ The Question You Must Ask
Were any validation studies done in non-European populations? Different pathogens? Different healthcare resources?
🔍 The Hidden Truth

Post-implementation studies in diverse settings revealed:

  • European ICUs: c-statistic = 0.82 (as reported)
  • US academic centers: c-statistic = 0.76 (acceptable)
  • Asian hospitals: c-statistic = 0.64 (poor)
  • African hospitals: c-statistic = 0.58 (failed)
  • The reason: Different pathogens, comorbidities, lab availability, treatment resources

The model was not transportable. It worked where it was developed, but failed where it was most needed.

⚖️ Two Paths
❌ What They Did

Pooled only similar populations. Assumed transportability. No geographic validation. Recommended global use.

Result: Failed predictions. Wrong treatments. Patients harmed in low-resource settings.

✅ What META-SPRINT Would Do

Stratify validations by setting. Assess geographic heterogeneity. Require validation in target population. Flag transportability limits.

Result: Honest limits stated. Local validation required. Appropriate model selection.

📜 The Lesson
A model that works in one setting may fail in another. Always stratify validations by geography and setting. Never assume transportability without evidence.
🤔 Reflect: Have you assessed heterogeneity by validation setting? Have you examined transportability to your target population?
Story Four
The Predictor That Wasn't
"Every study shows this biomarker predicts outcomes!" But why are only positive studies published?
🌅 The Beginning

A meta-analysis found 25 studies all showing biomarker Y predicted poor survival. Pooled HR: 2.4 (95% CI: 2.0-2.9). Forest plot looked perfect — all studies on the same side!

Pharmaceutical companies developed tests. Guidelines recommended measurement. Patients were stratified by biomarker Y.

❓ The Question You Must Ask
Why are all 25 studies positive? Is this biologically plausible, or is there publication bias? Where are the null studies?
🔍 The Hidden Truth

Careful investigation revealed massive publication bias:

  • Funnel plot: Severely asymmetric — small negative studies missing
  • Egger test: p < 0.001 — strong evidence of bias
  • Trim-and-fill: Estimated 15 missing studies with null results
  • Adjusted estimate: HR = 1.3 [0.9-1.8] — NOT SIGNIFICANT

The "consistent" effect was publication bias. Null studies never got published. The true effect was modest at best.

⚖️ Two Paths
❌ What They Did

Pooled all published studies. No publication bias assessment. Concluded biomarker Y is strongly prognostic.

Result: Expensive testing. Inflated expectations. Wrong treatment decisions based on false positive.

✅ What META-SPRINT Would Do

Assess publication bias (funnel, Egger, trim-fill). Search grey literature. Contact authors. Adjust estimate for bias.

Result: Honest uncertainty. Caution about biomarker. Better resource allocation.

📜 The Lesson
Publication bias is rampant in prognostic factor research. Positive studies get published; null studies disappear. Always assess for publication bias. Search grey literature aggressively.
🤔 Reflect: Have you assessed your funnel plot? Have you searched for unpublished null studies?
📜 The Four Lessons
1. Development performance is optimistic fiction.
Always require external validation. Check events per variable. Pool validation c-statistics separately.
2. Unadjusted and adjusted effects tell different stories.
Pool them separately. Independent prognostic value requires proper adjustment.
3. Models may not transport to new settings.
Stratify validations by geography. Never assume transportability without evidence.
4. Publication bias inflates prognostic effects.
Assess funnel plots. Search grey literature. Adjust for missing null studies.

📖 Worked Example: Oncology DOSE
LEARNING

Walk through a mini-DOSE example: dose-response for blood pressure reduction across 4 antihypertensive dose levels.

Scenario

Compare efficacy of 4 treatments for major depression: Placebo, Drug A, Drug B, Drug C. We have 12 RCTs with the following direct comparisons:

  • 5 trials: Placebo vs Drug A
  • 3 trials: Placebo vs Drug B
  • 2 trials: Drug A vs Drug B
  • 2 trials: Drug A vs Drug C
1 Study Registry
Node 1: Placebo | Node 2: Drug A | Node 3: Drug B | Node 4: Drug C
2 Check Connectivity
✅ Accuracy is connected (all tests reachable)
⚠️ Note: No direct Placebo vs Drug C trials — this comparison is indirect only
3 Assess Applicability
Check effect modifiers across comparisons:
• Severity: All trials in moderate-severe depression ✅
• Age: Mean age 35-45 across all trials ✅
• Duration: 8-12 weeks ✅
→ Applicability: Low concern
4 Pool Dose-Response Curves
Using frequentist random-effects (netmeta in R):
Drug A vs Placebo: OR 1.45 [1.20, 1.75]
Drug B vs Placebo: OR 1.38 [1.10, 1.73]
Drug C vs Placebo: OR 1.52 [1.15, 2.01] ← indirect!
5 Check Consistency
Global test (design-by-treatment): p = 0.42 → No evidence of threshold effects ✅
Node-splitting for A vs B: Direct OR 1.05, Indirect OR 1.08, p = 0.85 ✅
6 PROBAST Assessment (Drug C vs Placebo)
• Within-study bias: Some concerns (3/4 trials high RoB)
• Indirectness: Low concern
• Imprecision: Some concerns (CI crosses 1.5 threshold)
• Heterogeneity: Low concern (I² = 28%)
• Incoherence: Low concern
→ Overall: Moderate confidence
7 Summary Dose-Response Decision
P-scores: Drug C (72%), Drug A (68%), Drug B (45%), Placebo (15%)
✅ PROBAST not "Low" for key contrasts
✅ No severe incoherence
✅ Applicability OK
→ Summary Dose-Response CAN be reported (with caution language)
✅ Conclusion: All 4 drugs show benefit vs placebo. Drug C ranks highest but evidence is indirect only. Recommend Drug A or B as first-line (more direct evidence). Drug C may be considered with acknowledgment of indirect evidence.
