Module 1: Health Technology Assessment Fundamentals (10 min)

What is Health Technology Assessment?

HTA synthesizes clinical effectiveness, cost-effectiveness, and budget impact evidence to inform healthcare coverage and reimbursement decisions.

Key Components:

  • ICER: Incremental Cost-Effectiveness Ratio — cost per QALY gained
  • QALY: Quality-Adjusted Life Year — combines survival and quality of life
  • WTP Threshold: Willingness-to-Pay threshold (e.g., $50,000-$150,000/QALY)
  • Budget Impact: Total cost to healthcare system over time horizon
⚠️ Common Mistake: Focusing only on ICER without considering budget impact, equity, or implementation feasibility. HTA requires multi-criteria assessment.
✅ Example HTA Question:
Technology: New oral cancer drug vs current standard of care
ICER: $85,000/QALY gained
Budget Impact: £120M over 5 years
Decision: Recommend with managed access agreement?

Module 2: Economic Modeling for HTA (10 min)

Types of Economic Models in HTA

Economic models translate clinical evidence into cost-effectiveness estimates. Choose the right model structure for your decision problem.

Model Selection Steps:

  • Define: Decision problem, perspective, time horizon
  • Structure: Choose model type (decision tree, Markov, DES)
  • Populate: Input clinical, utility, and cost parameters
  • Validate: Face validity, internal validation, external validation
Model TypeWhen to Use
Decision TreeShort time horizon, simple pathways
Markov ModelChronic disease, recurring events
Discrete Event SimulationPatient heterogeneity, complex pathways
Partitioned SurvivalOncology, progression-free survival data
🚫 Common Pitfalls: Wrong time horizon, inappropriate discounting, ignoring uncertainty, structural assumptions not justified.

Module 3: Synthesizing Evidence for HTA (10 min)

How to Synthesize Evidence for Cost-Effectiveness?

HTA requires synthesizing clinical, economic, and patient preference data. Use network meta-analysis when direct comparisons are lacking.

Key Principle: ICER uncertainty propagates from clinical, utility, and cost inputs. Probabilistic sensitivity analysis (PSA) is essential.

Uncertainty SourceHow to AddressPresentation
Clinical effectivenessMeta-analysis with credible intervalsForest plots, NMA network
Utility valuesPooled from EQ-5D studiesUtility decrement ranges
CostsMicro-costing, sensitivity rangesTornado diagrams
StructuralAlternative model structuresScenario analyses
💡 Probabilistic Sensitivity Analysis:
1. Assign distributions to all uncertain parameters
2. Run 10,000+ Monte Carlo iterations
3. Generate cost-effectiveness acceptability curve (CEAC)
4. Report probability of being cost-effective at WTP threshold

Module 4: Key HTA Outputs & Interpretation (5 min)

Key HTA Outputs:

  • ICER: Incremental cost per QALY gained — primary cost-effectiveness measure
  • Net Monetary Benefit: QALYs × WTP - Costs — alternative to ICER
  • Budget Impact: Total cost to payer over defined time horizon
  • CEAC: Probability of cost-effectiveness across WTP thresholds

What ICER tells you:

  • Additional cost required to gain one QALY
  • Whether technology is "worth it" vs WTP threshold
  • Relative value compared to alternatives

What ICER doesn't tell you:

  • Affordability (need budget impact analysis)
  • Equity implications (who benefits/pays)
  • Implementation feasibility
🚫 CAUTION WITH ICER IF:
• QALY loss scenario (negative denominator)
• High structural uncertainty in model
• Wide confidence intervals on clinical inputs
• Short time horizon with extrapolation beyond data

ROBINS-E Quick Reference:
  • Confounding: Were key prognostic factors balanced or adjusted?
  • Selection: Was study entry related to both exposure and outcome?
  • Exposure: Was treatment/exposure accurately classified?
  • Post-exposure: Were co-interventions balanced across groups?
  • Missing data: Was follow-up complete? Differential censoring?
  • Outcome: Was outcome measurement valid and blinded?
  • Reporting: Was the analysis pre-specified?

Story One
The ICER That Ignored Uncertainty
"ICER = $45,000/QALY! Clearly cost-effective..." But was it really?
🌅 The Beginning

A manufacturer submitted an HTA dossier with base case ICER: $45,000/QALY. Below the $50,000 threshold! "Cost-effective and ready for reimbursement."

The health ministry approved it. The budget allocated $200 million annually. Patients celebrated access.

❓ The Question You Must Ask
What does the probabilistic sensitivity analysis show? What is the probability of being cost-effective at the WTP threshold?
🔍 The Hidden Truth

An independent reanalysis with proper PSA revealed:

  • 95% CI for ICER: $15,000 to $180,000/QALY — massive uncertainty
  • Probability cost-effective at $50K: Only 52% — essentially a coin flip
  • Key driver: Utility values from tiny, biased study (n=23)
  • Sensitivity: Using validated EQ-5D, ICER jumped to $95,000/QALY

The "clearly cost-effective" drug was actually a high-risk gamble with enormous uncertainty.

⚖️ Two Paths
❌ What They Did

Reported only base case ICER. Hid uncertainty. Used favorable utility source. Ignored PSA results.

Result: $200M/year spent on uncertain value. Opportunity cost of displaced programs. Budget pressure.

✅ What META-SPRINT Would Do

Mandatory PSA with CEAC. Report probability of cost-effectiveness. Sensitivity to utility source. Value of information analysis.

Result: Informed decision with acknowledged uncertainty. Managed access agreement. Coverage with evidence development.

📜 The Lesson
A point estimate ICER is meaningless without uncertainty. Always run PSA. Always report CEAC. An ICER below threshold with 51% probability is NOT a confident decision.
🤔 Reflect: What is the 95% CI around your ICER? What is the probability of cost-effectiveness at your WTP threshold?
Story Two
The Budget That Broke the System
"ICER is excellent! Only $30,000/QALY..." But can we afford it?
🌅 The Beginning

A new gene therapy showed ICER of $30,000/QALY. Far below the £30,000 NICE threshold. "Clearly good value!" The committee recommended approval.

The one-time treatment cost: $2.1 million per patient. Eligible population: 5,000 patients. Nobody did the math.

❓ The Question You Must Ask
What is the total budget impact? Can the healthcare system absorb this cost? What gets displaced?
🔍 The Hidden Truth

Budget impact analysis revealed catastrophic affordability issues:

  • Year 1 cost: $10.5 billion (5,000 × $2.1M) — 15% of total drug budget
  • Displaced programs: Cancer screening cut, mental health services reduced
  • Net health loss: QALYs lost from displaced care exceeded QALYs gained
  • System strain: Other essential medicines rationed

The "cost-effective" therapy was unaffordable. Good ICER ≠ good decision.

⚖️ Two Paths
❌ What They Did

Approved based on ICER alone. Ignored budget impact. No affordability assessment. Assumed "cost-effective = must fund."

Result: Budget crisis. Program displacement. Net health loss for population.

✅ What META-SPRINT Would Do

Mandatory budget impact analysis. Affordability threshold. Staggered access. Outcomes-based pricing. Portfolio approach.

Result: Sustainable access. Managed introduction. Protected existing programs.

📜 The Lesson
Cost-effectiveness is not affordability. A low ICER with massive budget impact can harm more patients than it helps. Always do budget impact analysis. Consider opportunity cost.
🤔 Reflect: What is the total budget impact of your technology? What existing programs might be displaced?
Story Three
The Model That Forgot Time
"5-year model shows excellent value!" But what about year 6?
🌅 The Beginning

A manufacturer submitted a 5-year Markov model. ICER: $40,000/QALY. Clean, simple, favorable. The HTA committee was pleased.

Treatment was for a chronic disease. Patients would take it for life. Average life expectancy: 25 more years.

❓ The Question You Must Ask
Is the time horizon appropriate? What happens beyond the model horizon? Are costs and benefits captured?
🔍 The Hidden Truth

Extending the model to lifetime horizon revealed:

  • Drug costs: Continued for 25 years, not 5 — $500K more per patient
  • Adverse events: Accumulated over time, eroding QALY gains
  • Lifetime ICER: $120,000/QALY — 3× the submitted value
  • Discounting error: Benefits front-loaded, costs spread over time

The short time horizon hid the true lifetime cost. The model was technically valid but strategically misleading.

⚖️ Two Paths
❌ What They Did

Accepted 5-year model for chronic disease. Didn't question time horizon. Ignored lifetime costs.

Result: Massive budget overrun. Drug became unaffordable. Access restricted later.

✅ What META-SPRINT Would Do

Require lifetime horizon for chronic conditions. Sensitivity on time horizon. Validate extrapolation assumptions.

Result: Accurate lifetime cost estimate. Appropriate pricing negotiations. Sustainable access.

📜 The Lesson
Time horizon must capture all relevant differences. A 5-year model for a lifetime disease is misleading. Always match time horizon to disease duration. Always test sensitivity to horizon.
🤔 Reflect: Does your time horizon capture all costs and benefits? What happens beyond your model horizon?
Story Four
The Utility That Wasn't Universal
"Utility gain of 0.15! Life-changing improvement!" But whose values?
🌅 The Beginning

A treatment for rare disease showed utility improvement from 0.60 to 0.75. "15% improvement in quality of life!" ICER: $55,000/QALY.

The utility came from a disease-specific instrument developed by the manufacturer. Patients reported dramatic improvements.

❓ The Question You Must Ask
Where did utility values come from? Are they from EQ-5D with population tariff? Are they comparable across decisions?
🔍 The Hidden Truth

Re-analysis using NICE reference case (EQ-5D-5L) revealed:

  • Disease-specific utility: 0.75 (manufacturer's instrument)
  • EQ-5D-5L utility: 0.65 — only 0.05 improvement, not 0.15
  • Reason: Disease-specific tool captured symptoms not valued by general public
  • Corrected ICER: $165,000/QALY — 3× higher

The utility inflation made a poor value drug look cost-effective. Apples were compared to oranges.

⚖️ Two Paths
❌ What They Did

Accepted disease-specific utility. Didn't require EQ-5D. Compared to other drugs using different scales.

Result: Inconsistent decisions. Rare disease drugs favored. Other patients displaced.

✅ What META-SPRINT Would Do

Require EQ-5D as reference case. Map disease-specific to EQ-5D if needed. Transparent utility sources.

Result: Consistent decisions across disease areas. Fair comparison. Transparent trade-offs.

📜 The Lesson
Utility values must be comparable across decisions. Disease-specific instruments inflate value. Always use EQ-5D or map to it. Consistency enables fair resource allocation.
🤔 Reflect: Where do your utility values come from? Are they EQ-5D with population tariff? Are they comparable to other HTAs?
📜 The Four Lessons
1. Point estimate ICERs are meaningless without uncertainty.
Always run PSA. Always report CEAC. Quantify decision uncertainty.
2. Cost-effectiveness is not affordability.
Always do budget impact. Consider opportunity cost. What gets displaced?
3. Time horizon must match disease duration.
Lifetime horizon for chronic conditions. Test sensitivity. Don't hide costs.
4. Utility values must be comparable.
Use EQ-5D reference case. Map if needed. Enable fair comparisons.

HTA-Specific Concepts

Applicability Assumption

The assumption that indirect comparisons are valid because studies are sufficiently similar in effect modifiers. If populations differ systematically, indirect evidence may be biased.

Sensitivity Analysis Types

TypeDescriptionApproach
One-waySingle parameter variationTornado diagrams
ProbabilisticAll parameters vary simultaneouslyMonte Carlo simulation, CEAC
StructuralModel assumptionsAlternative structures, time horizons

ROBINS-E Domains

DomainQuestion
Within-study biasRisk of bias in included studies?
Reporting biasMissing studies/outcomes?
IndirectnessApplicability to PICO?
ImprecisionConfidence interval width?
HeterogeneityBetween-study variability?
IncoherenceDirect vs indirect agreement?

When to Flag ICER Uncertainty

Flag high uncertainty if ANY:
• Probability of cost-effectiveness < 70% at WTP threshold
• ICER confidence interval crosses WTP threshold
• Structural sensitivity changes conclusions
• Key parameters based on low-quality evidence

📖 Worked Example: Oncology HTA
LEARNING

Walk through a mini-HTA comparing 4 treatments for overall survival in lung cancer.

Scenario

Compare efficacy of 4 treatments for major depression: Placebo, Drug A, Drug B, Drug C. We have 12 RCTs with the following direct comparisons:

  • 5 trials: Placebo vs Drug A
  • 3 trials: Placebo vs Drug B
  • 2 trials: Drug A vs Drug B
  • 2 trials: Drug A vs Drug C
1 Study Registry
Node 1: Placebo | Node 2: Drug A | Node 3: Drug B | Node 4: Drug C
2 Check Connectivity
✅ Accuracy is connected (all tests reachable)
⚠️ Note: No direct Placebo vs Drug C trials — this comparison is indirect only
3 Assess Applicability
Check effect modifiers across comparisons:
• Severity: All trials in moderate-severe depression ✅
• Age: Mean age 35-45 across all trials ✅
• Duration: 8-12 weeks ✅
→ Applicability: Low concern
4 Pool Cost-Effectivenesss
Using frequentist random-effects (netmeta in R):
Drug A vs Placebo: OR 1.45 [1.20, 1.75]
Drug B vs Placebo: OR 1.38 [1.10, 1.73]
Drug C vs Placebo: OR 1.52 [1.15, 2.01] ← indirect!
5 Check Consistency
Global test (design-by-treatment): p = 0.42 → No evidence of threshold effects ✅
Node-splitting for A vs B: Direct OR 1.05, Indirect OR 1.08, p = 0.85 ✅
6 Cost-Effectiveness Assessment (Drug C vs SOC)
• Base case ICER: $42,000/QALY
• PSA: 78% probability cost-effective at $50,000 WTP
• Key drivers: Progression-free survival, drug cost
• Structural sensitivity: ICER range $35,000-$58,000
→ Overall: Likely cost-effective with moderate uncertainty
7 Cost-Effectiveness Conclusion
ICER results by comparator:
• Drug C vs SOC: $42,000/QALY (78% prob at $50k WTP)
• Drug A vs SOC: $38,000/QALY (85% prob at $50k WTP)
✅ PSA supports conclusions (probability > 70%)
✅ Structural sensitivity robust
→ Cost-effectiveness conclusions CAN be drawn (with uncertainty acknowledgment)
✅ Conclusion: Both Drug A and C are cost-effective vs SOC at $50,000/QALY. Drug A has higher probability of cost-effectiveness. Budget impact analysis shows $15M annual cost. Recommend Drug A as first-line; Drug C as alternative.
